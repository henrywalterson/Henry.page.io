Mobile App · 2024

Redesigning the onboarding flow so new users reach their first "aha moment" in under 60 seconds.

DEMOPROJECT

A complete rethink of the first-run experience for a B2C productivity app — cutting average time-to-value from 4 minutes to 48 seconds across iOS and Android.

Role

Lead Product Designer

Responsibilities

UX Research Interaction Design Prototyping Usability Testing

Company

Demo Corp

Product

DemoApp — Task Manager

Timeline

8 weeks · Q2 2024

Context

DemoApp had strong retention after week two — but was losing 68% of users before they ever got there.

DemoApp is a cross-platform productivity tool used by freelancers and small teams. The core product was well-regarded by long-term users, but acquisition data showed a sharp drop-off in the first session — most users never created their first task.

The business had invested heavily in paid acquisition, which made the onboarding leak increasingly expensive. This project was scoped as a 0→1 redesign of the onboarding flow only, leaving the core product untouched.

Drop-off funnel — Session 1 data, March 2024

The Problem

Users were asked to configure before they were shown any value.

The existing flow opened with a 6-screen setup wizard — asking for team size, use case, notification preferences, and integrations — before the user had ever seen what the product could do for them. By screen 3, over half had already churned.

The core tension: the team believed configuration was necessary for personalisation. But users had no frame of reference to make those decisions meaningfully. They were being asked to commit to a product they hadn't yet understood.

"You can't personalise an experience for someone who doesn't yet know what they want from it."
Research

Five session recordings and three user interviews pointed to the same moment of abandonment.

Without access to a full research budget, I ran lightweight guerrilla research: 5 moderated session recordings with users matching the acquisition persona (freelancers, 25–38), 3 contextual interviews with existing power users, and a competitive teardown of 6 onboarding flows in adjacent productivity tools.

Every session showed the same break point: users paused, re-read the question, then closed the app. The decision cost was higher than the perceived reward. No one understood why the question mattered before they'd seen the product work.

Affinity map — 3 interviews, 47 observations clustered into 6 themes

"The question isn't how to make the setup wizard shorter — it's whether it should exist at all in session one."
Design Decisions

Three structural moves that changed the first-session experience.

Show the product immediately — defer setup entirely.

Instead of a wizard, users land directly in a pre-populated workspace with sample tasks and a guided highlight tour. Setup questions are moved to a progressive disclosure flow triggered after the user completes their first task — when they have enough context to answer meaningfully.

Replace onboarding screens with a single persistent coach mark layer.

Multi-screen onboarding creates a context switch — users must re-orient when they finally reach the product. A single contextual layer (3 coach marks max) teaches by doing, inside the real product, without breaking flow. This also means zero additional screens to maintain.

Define "aha moment" as first task created, and build the entire flow toward it.

The previous flow had no explicit success state for session one. We defined the aha moment as the user creating and completing their first task — and redesigned every step as a funnel toward that moment. The sample workspace was pre-seeded to make that first task a one-tap action.

The redesign shipped as three connected surfaces:

Pre-populated workspace — sample project with 4 tasks seeded on first launch, removing the blank-slate problem entirely

3-step coach mark layer — contextual highlights for Create, Complete, and Share — triggered on first interaction, dismissible at any time

Deferred setup flow — team size and use-case questions surfaced post-first-task via a bottom sheet, not a blocker

Empty state redesign — after sample tasks are cleared, a persistent empty state with a single CTA replaces the blank canvas

Coach mark — step 1 of 3

Deferred setup — triggered after first task completed

Reflection

The hardest part wasn't the design — it was convincing the team to remove things.

Every screen we removed had an internal stakeholder who had advocated for it. The permission-request screen ("we need this for notifications"), the use-case question ("marketing needs this for segmentation"), the team-size question ("sales needs this to qualify leads"). Each had a legitimate business reason to exist — just not in session one.

Before (6 screens, avg. 4m 12s) → After (0 screens, avg. 48s to first task)

"Simplicity isn't the absence of features — it's knowing which features belong in which moment."

The biggest shift in my own thinking was moving away from "what does the user need to know?" toward "what does the user need to feel?" in the first session. Confidence and competence come from doing, not from being told. The coach marks only worked because users were already inside the product when they appeared.

If I had more time, I would have tested a version where the sample workspace used real content imported from the user's calendar or existing tools — reducing setup to zero rather than deferring it. That's the direction I'd push in a second iteration.

This project was completed without access to a full analytics suite or A/B testing infrastructure. Outcome metrics (48s time-to-value, 68% → 31% drop-off) are from a limited beta rollout of ~200 users over 3 weeks — directionally strong but not statistically conclusive.
